The Roots

Where it all begins

Jewish tradition does not treat the earth as backdrop. It treats the earth as covenant. These are the values that anchor everything we build.

Tikkun Olam

Repair of the world — not as metaphor but as practice. Every seed planted is an act of repair.

Bal Tashchit

Do not destroy needlessly. The Torah's first environmental law. The root of every recycling sign we hang.

L'dor V'dor

From generation to generation. The obligation to transmit what we've learned — and what we've grown.

Shmirat HaAdamah

Guarding the earth. An ancient obligation that has never been more urgent.

These values were tested in soil before they were written in text.

The Trunk

Beth El is the shared structure

"I did not find this world desolate when I entered it. My ancestors planted for me. I plant for those who come after me."

Honi the Circle Maker, upon seeing an old man plant a carob tree · Talmud, Ta'anit 23a

Honi asked the old man why he was planting — a carob tree takes 70 years to bear fruit. He would never taste it. The man answered without hesitation. He planted anyway.

Beth El is that tree. Families with toddlers and teenagers. Empty nesters who remember when the building was new. Kids planting their first seed. Teens running their first event. Grandparents watching it all happen. The trunk holds all of it — not because it is perfect, but because it has been here, and because people keep showing up to tend it.

But Honi's story has a shadow. When he woke after 70 years, the carob tree was there — fruiting, exactly as promised. His hevruta partners were gone. His community was gone. No one knew him anymore. And the Talmud says he died of grief.

The tree survived. The community did not tend each other. We are planting both.

The Seeds

What we leave behind for whoever comes next

A seed doesn't know what it will become. It carries the instructions forward anyway. The goal of this initiative is not programs. It is culture — the kind that lives on after any one of us has moved on.
